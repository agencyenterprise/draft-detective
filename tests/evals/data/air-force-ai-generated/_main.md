United States Air Force Flying Mission Readiness: A Strategic Assessment and Policy Roadmap

**Executive Summary**

The United States Air Force faces a multidimensional readiness crisis that threatens its ability to generate combat-ready sorties at the scale and tempo required to maintain air superiority against peer adversaries. With fleet-wide mission capable rates at a historic low of 62% in FY2024—leaving nearly 1,900 aircraft unavailable on any given day—and a persistent shortage of approximately 1,850 pilots, the service confronts systemic challenges across pilot production and retention, aircraft availability, and critical infrastructure.[[1](#fn1)] [[2]](#fn2)

This assessment identifies key vulnerabilities across three operational pillars and proposes evidence-based, non-partisan policy recommendations to restore combat effectiveness. The analysis reveals that decades of deferred investment, compounded by an aging fleet averaging 32 years old and a $49.5 billion infrastructure maintenance backlog, have created cascading readiness shortfalls that require immediate attention. With China rapidly expanding its military capabilities and the Air Force operating at the smallest force structure in its 78-year history (5,025 aircraft), addressing these systemic gaps is imperative to maintaining deterrence and warfighting capacity.[[3](#fn3)] [[4](#fn4)] [[1]](#fn1)

# Current Readiness Landscape (2024-2026)

- 1. **Pilot & Aircrew Training**

**Production Status**

The Air Force's undergraduate pilot training enterprise continues to fall short of validated requirements despite significant modernization efforts. Air Education and Training Command (AETC) produced approximately 1,350 pilots in FY2023, missing its goal of 1,470. While the service has established an ambitious target of 1,500 pilots annually beginning in FY2026 through the new Future Undergraduate Pilot Training (FUPT) program, the pipeline remains constrained by multiple bottlenecks.[[5](#fn5)] [[6]](#fn6)

The FUPT initiative represents a fundamental restructuring of pilot production, outsourcing initial flight training to four civilian flying universities where students complete 110 flying hours over 139 days to earn FAA private pilot licenses before transitioning to military T-6 and T-38 training at Air Force bases. This hybrid model is expected to produce just 100 pilots in 2025, ramping to 750 in 2026, with full implementation reaching 1,500 annually by 2027. Each of the four UPT bases—Columbus, Laughlin, Sheppard, and Vance—must sustain 75 student sorties daily to graduate approximately 425 pilots annually, a threshold complicated by aging trainer aircraft and infrastructure constraints.[[7](#fn7)] [[5]](#fn5)

The T-38 Talon fleet, with an average age approaching 60 years, posted a mission capable rate of just 55% in FY2024, down from 63% in 2021. Its designated replacement, the Boeing T-7A Red Hawk, has suffered repeated delays. Originally scheduled for initial operational capability in 2023, the program has been pushed back multiple times due to ejection system design flaws,

flight control software issues, and supply chain disruptions. The production decision, now delayed until 2026, means IOC will not occur until November 2027 at the earliest—four years behind the original schedule. The Air Force has attempted to mitigate these delays by purchasing four additional production-representative test aircraft to accelerate qualification testing, but chronic quality problems at Boeing's St. Louis facility continue to create uncertainty.

[8] [9] [10] [1]

The T-6A Texan II primary trainer experienced an 8.57 percentage point decline in mission capable rates between 2023 and 2024, exacerbating throughput constraints in the early [ph](#fn8)a[s](#fn9)e[s o](#fn10)f [t](#fn1)raining. Compounding these material challenges is a critical shortage of simulator instructors at UPT bases, forcing uniformed instructor pilots to backfill positions, which reduces the number of experienced aviators available for operational squadrons.[[7]](#fn7)

**Absorption and Experience Deficit**

Even when the Air Force successfully produces new pilots, its capacity to absorb them into operational squadrons and develop them into experienced combat aviators remains severely constrained. Fighter squadrons operate most effectively when 60% of their pilots are experienced—defined as having completed approximately 250 sorties in their mission aircraft and achieved four-ship flight lead qualification. Currently, fewer than half of all active duty fighter squadrons maintain this healthy experience ratio. [[11](#fn11)] [[12]](#fn12)

When squadron experience levels drop by just 10%, flying program efficiency falls by approximately 15%, as the remaining experienced pilots must dedicate more flight hours to training inexperienced wingmen rather than executing advanced tactical scenarios. This creates a vicious cycle: as experienced pilots depart, the remaining instructors face higher workloads, diminished quality of life, and increased burnout—factors that further drive retention losses.[[12]](#fn12)

The Air Force has attempted to address absorption constraints through Total Force Integration, leveraging the 495th Fighter Group at Shaw Air Force Base to pair active duty pilots with highly experienced Air National Guard and Reserve instructor pilots. This model allows new pilots to gain experience in ANG/Reserve units that maintain higher concentrations of senior aviators, but expanding this approach is limited by the finite number of available billets and the willingness of experienced pilots to transition to reserve status.[[11]](#fn11)

**Retention Crisis**

Pilot retention represents perhaps the most intractable challenge facing Air Force aviation. The service has maintained a shortage of approximately 2,000 pilots for over a decade, with 1,142 fighter pilot billets unfilled in FY2024 alone. Multiple factors drive this exodus, but competition from commercial airlines stands out as the primary structural challenge.[[2](#fn2)] [[13](#fn13)] [[6]](#fn6)

The civilian airline industry is experiencing unprecedented demand for pilots. Boeing's 2025 Pilot and Technician Outlook projects a need for 2.4 million aviation professionals globally by 2044, with the commercial airline market capable of absorbing the Air Force's entire pilot inventory multiple times over. A 2013 FAA regulation requiring 1,500 flight hours for civilian airline pilots—but only 750 hours for military-trained aviators—has made Air Force pilots even more attractive to commercial carriers, as most military pilots far exceed this threshold upon completing their initial service commitment.[[14](#fn14)] [[15](#fn15)] [[16]](#fn16)

Beyond financial incentives, quality-of-life factors significantly influence retention decisions. The Air Force Force Generation (AFFORGEN) model, implemented in 2022 to replace the 20-year-old Air Expeditionary Force structure, established predictable 24-month deployment cycles divided into four six-month phases: Prepare, Certify, Available to Commit, and Reset. While designed to reduce uncertainty and improve readiness for high-end conflict, the system has struggled with mid-cycle assignment turbulence, where pilots receive permanent change of station orders or out-of-cycle deployment taskers that disrupt unit cohesion.[[17](#fn17)] [[18](#fn18)] [[19]](#fn19)

High operational tempo remains a persistent complaint. Fighter pilots in Ready Aircrew Program (RAP) coded units must fly a minimum of nine sorties per month for inexperienced pilots and eight for experienced aviators to maintain combat mission ready status. However, many squadrons struggle to meet even these minimum thresholds due to aircraft availability constraints, resulting in pilots who are technically unqualified for their wartime missions. When pilots do fly, they average just six sorties per month in some platforms—such as the F-35A-falling far short of the historical standard of three sorties per week (approximately 12-13 per month) needed to develop and maintain combat proficiency.[[20]](#fn20)

Administrative burdens compound these frustrations. Despite a 2016 initiative that eliminated, reassigned, or reduced 29 of 61 additional duties specified in Air Force Instruction 38-206, pilots consistently report that non-flying administrative tasks consume excessive time and reduce focus on core mission activities. The establishment of Commander Support Staff positions was intended to offload these duties, but manning shortages mean many squadrons still lack adequate administrative support.[[21](#fn21)] [[22]](#fn22)

The Air Force has responded with enhanced financial incentives. The Rated Officer Retention Demonstration Program, introduced in 2023, offers pilots whose initial 10-year contracts expire in FY2024 or FY2025 annual bonuses ranging from $35,000 to $50,000 for commitments of 4 to 12 additional years. Fighter, bomber, mobility, search-and-rescue, special operations, and U-2 pilots can earn up to $600,000 over 12 years. The FY2026 budget requests an additional $71.2 million for officer aircrew incentive pay and $15.6 million for retention bonuses, reflecting higher-than-anticipated participation rates. However, these programs have proven expensive relative to their effectiveness, as evidenced by the Air Force closing the FY2025 Selective Retention Bonus program early due to budget exhaustion—a reflection of strong enlisted retention but also the program's high cost relative to available funding.[[23](#fn23)] [[24](#fn24)] [[25]](#fn25)

# Aircraft Readiness

**Mission Capable Rate Crisis**

The Air Force's fleet-wide mission capable rate of 62% in FY2024 represents the lowest readiness level in recent memory and marks a steady decline from 78% in 2012. This means that on any given day, approximately 1,900 aircraft out of the service's 5,025-strong fleet are unavailable for missions—a devastating capability gap when measured against potential requirements in a major regional contingency.[[26](#fn26)] [[1]](#fn1)

The steepness and breadth of this decline alarm senior leadership. At the 2025 Air and Space Forces Association Warfare Symposium, Air Force Chief of Staff General David Allvin presented data showing average aircraft age increasing from 17 years in 1994 to nearly 32 years in 2024,

while aircraft availability plummeted from 73% to 54%. The convergence of these trendlines—aging fleets becoming increasingly difficult to maintain—portends further deterioration absent aggressive intervention.[1]

Platform-specific readiness tells an even more concerning story: **Fighter Aircraft:**

- F-35A: Mission capable rate collapsed from 68.8% in 2021 to 51.5% in 2024, despite being the newest fighter in the inventory and the cornerstone of future air superiority. The F-35 Joint Program Office launched a "war on readiness" in 2023 to address chronic spare parts shortages, inadequate depot capacity, and insufficient maintainer training, but progress has been slow. [[1]](#fn1)
- F-22: The 5th-generation air superiority fighter posted a dismal 49% mission capable rate as recently as 2017, though more current figures were unavailable.[[27]](#fn27)
- F-16C: Declined from 71.9% (2021) to 64% (2024); the F-16D variant fared worse, dropping from 69% to 59%.[[1]](#fn1)
- A-10: Fell from 72% (2021) to 67% (2024), even as the aircraft faces planned retirement.[[1]](#fn1)

**Bomber Fleet:**

- B-52: Slipped from 59% (2021) to 54% (2024), with the aging Stratofortress—average age over 60 years—requiring increasingly expensive maintenance.[[1]](#fn1)
- B-1: While specific 2024 figures weren't available, the B-1 faces planned retirement before the B-21 Raider reaches operational capability, creating a dangerous "bathtub effect" that will temporarily reduce bomber capacity by 30% as an entire squadron (10 aircraft) is inactivated at once.[[28]](#fn28)

**Mobility and ISR:**

- KC-46: Mission capable rate dropped from 71% (2021) to 61% (2024), creating tanker shortfalls that constrain power projection.[[1]](#fn1)
- CV-22 Osprey: Catastrophic decline from 51% (2021) to 30% (2024), driven by faulty clutch components, design deficiencies, and fatal crashes that resulted in multiple groundings.[[1]](#fn1)

**Root Causes: Supply Chain and Maintenance Workforce**

Two interconnected factors drive this readiness crisis: spare parts shortages and maintenance workforce challenges.

The F-35 exemplifies the supply chain problem. A 2023 Government Accountability Office report identified critical shortfalls: insufficient spare parts inventory, inadequate technical data for repairs, improperly trained maintainers, and depot repair capacity well below requirements. Air Force officials report that F-35 parts cannibalization—removing components from one aircraft to repair another—has become routine due to supply chain bottlenecks. F-35 depot capacity will not reach full operational capability until 2028, meaning readiness constraints will persist for years.[[29](#fn29)] [[30](#fn30)] [[1]](#fn1)

To improve F-35 supply chain responsiveness, the Defense Logistics Agency assumed warehousing responsibility for government-owned spares in 2020, transitioning more than 5,000 part numbers and 2 million parts from Lockheed Martin facilities into DLA warehouses positioned closer to maintenance activities. U.S. Transportation Command now manages global distribution, theoretically reducing delivery times. However, the transition has been complex, and readiness gains remain marginal.[[31](#fn31)] [[29]](#fn29)

The maintenance workforce presents equally daunting challenges. The Air Force employs approximately 86,000 2A aircraft maintainers across active, Guard, and Reserve components. While the service reduced its maintainer shortage from approximately 1,800 in 2023 to just 500 in 2024 through aggressive recruiting, this headline number obscures a critical experience deficit. Building experienced 5-level and 7-level maintainers takes years; recruiting alone cannot solve the problem in the near term.[[32](#fn32)] [[33](#fn33)] [[34]](#fn34)

Air Force Chief of Staff General Allvin acknowledged this reality: "We're recruiting ok, but it takes a while to build a 3-level into a 5- and 7-level." The shortage of experienced maintainers increases workload for those who remain, contributing to burnout and retention challenges. A 2023 Air Force Times investigation linked a five-year high in aviation mishaps during FY2023 in part to the maintainer shortage spread across an aging, high-demand fleet. [[34](#fn34)] [[32]](#fn32)

Competition from the civilian sector threatens to worsen these trends. The global aviation industry requires 710,000 new maintenance technicians by 2044, with the United States facing a projected shortage of 43,000 aviation mechanics by 2027—representing 27% of the total workforce. Eighty percent of the existing civilian A&P mechanic workforce is expected to retire within the next five to six years, and 27% of all FAA-certified mechanics are already 64 or older. This demographic cliff creates fierce competition for talent, with commercial aviation offering competitive salaries and more predictable schedules than military service.[[16](#fn16)] [[32]](#fn32)

**Predictive Maintenance: A Bright Spot**

One notable success story in the readiness portfolio is the Predictive Analytics and Decision Assistant (PANDA) system, co-developed by the Air Force Rapid Sustainment Office and C3 AI. Designated as the system of record for Condition Based Maintenance Plus (CBM+) in 2023, PANDA integrates artificial intelligence and machine learning across diverse aircraft maintenance data—historical records, onboard sensor telemetry, flight hours, environmental factors—to predict component failures before they occur.[[35](#fn35)] [[36]](#fn36)

Deployed on AWS GovCloud infrastructure, PANDA currently monitors more than 3,000 aircraft across 16 platforms with over 775 active users. In a proof-of-concept demonstration with the B-1 bomber fleet, PANDA achieved a complete elimination of unscheduled breaks for monitored systems and a 51% reduction in unscheduled maintenance man-hours. The Air Force awarded C3 AI a $13 million task order in June 2025 to expand PANDA deployment to additional aircraft platforms and develop new sensor-based algorithms under a broader $450 million contract vehicle.[[36](#fn36)] [[37](#fn37)] [[35]](#fn35)

While predictive maintenance cannot overcome fundamental spare parts shortages or maintainer workforce gaps, it represents a force multiplier that optimizes limited resources by

focusing maintenance actions on systems at genuine risk of failure, reducing over-maintenance, and improving parts positioning.

# Infrastructure

**Deferred Maintenance Backlog**

The Air Force faces an estimated $49.5 billion infrastructure repair backlog as of 2025, reflecting years of underfunding Facilities Sustainment, Restoration, and Modernization (FSRM) accounts. This figure encompasses only buildings—not runways, taxiways, fuel systems, ranges, or other critical infrastructure—making the true backlog substantially higher.[[4]](#fn4)

A 2024 Congressional Budget Office analysis of deferred maintenance across military services found the Air Force's buildings were in the best condition among the services, with the lowest deferred maintenance costs per building and per square foot. The Air Force had just two installations among the top 20 bases with the highest deferred maintenance costs, compared to eight for the Army and seven for the Navy. However, the cumulative $50 billion backlog across all military services (as of September 2020 data) represents approximately five years of typical FSRM budgets, indicating the magnitude of the problem.[[38](#fn38)] [[39]](#fn39)

Chronic underfunding drives this accumulation. In FY2025, continuing resolution constraints forced the Air Force to cut its FSRM account by $642 million "just to remain solvent," directly impacting quality of life for airmen, according to Air Force testimony before Congress. The FY2026 budget requests continued FSRM investment, but at levels that barely keep pace with current-year requirements, let alone address the accumulated backlog.[[40](#fn40)] [[41]](#fn41)

**Training Infrastructure Modernization**

Training ranges and simulators represent critical readiness enablers that have not kept pace with evolving threats. The Air Force operates hundreds of test and training ranges globally, but funding for modernization remains a recurring challenge. Current ranges and airspace constraints limit opportunities for realistic, high-end training essential for preparing airmen for contested environments against peer adversaries.[[42]](#fn42)

The Simulators Common Architecture Requirements and Standards (SCARS) program aims to update approximately 2,400 aircraft training simulators across 300 locations with common, open architecture and enhanced cybersecurity standards. This standardization will enable easier integration, more rapid updates, and improved interoperability across platforms. L3Harris leads a team including CAE USA, CymSTAR, Dell Technologies, and Leidos to implement these upgrades, with initial integration focusing on KC-135 and A-10 platforms at nine locations.[[43]](#fn43)

However, progress faces significant obstacles. Cybersecurity risk management framework requirements have halted funding and forward progress for some advanced simulator programs, creating a bureaucratic logjam that industry representatives describe as preventing movement on next-generation Live-Virtual-Constructive (LVC) training environments. As one industry official stated, "We are stopped with that risk management framework. It halts funding. It halts progress. It halts moving forward."[[44]](#fn44)

Virtual reality training offers promise for maintenance training. The National Guard Bureau distributed VR simulators to all Air National Guard wings in early 2024, with systems designed to replicate maintenance tasks when weather conditions or aircraft accessibility issues prevent hands-on training. The 190th Air Refueling Wing received five advanced VR simulators for KC-135 training, providing an alternative training medium that keeps maintainers current without competing for limited aircraft access. These systems support broader aircraft types beyond

unit-assigned platforms, enhancing adaptability for maintainers who may need to service transient aircraft. [[45]](#fn45)

**Base Housing and Quality of Life**

Infrastructure challenges extend beyond flight operations to encompass quality-of-life factors that directly influence retention. The Department of Defense has undertaken three major quality-of-life initiatives since 2021, addressing housing costs, child care access, spouse employment, and remote installation conditions.[[46]](#fn46)

Recent improvements include:

- Basic Allowance for Housing increased by an average of 18% over 2022 rates, with targeted increases in 28 military housing areas experiencing more than 20% rental cost spikes.[[46]](#fn46)
- Temporary Lodging Expense extended from 14 to 21 days for CONUS permanent change of station moves, and Temporary Lodging Allowance extended to 60 days for OCONUS departures (matching existing 60-day arrival allowance).[[46]](#fn46)
- Dependent Care Flexible Spending Accounts established, allowing service members to set aside up to $5,000 in pretax income for eligible dependent care expenses including child care, preschool, and summer programs.[[47](#fn47)] [[46]](#fn46)
- Health Care Flexible Spending Accounts introduced, enabling service members to contribute up to $3,200 annually in pre-tax dollars for out-of-pocket medical expenses including co-pays, orthodontia, prescriptions, and wellness treatments.[[46]](#fn46)
- Free wireless internet access expanded in unaccompanied housing.[[46]](#fn46)

Despite these improvements, housing quality and availability remain persistent concerns. Unaccompanied housing improvements focus on food access, internet connectivity, and deferred maintenance, but full funding of UH FSRM requirements continues to lag needs. A 2024 Quality of Life Panel recommended 100% funding of BAH (currently funded at lower levels in some areas), full funding of FSRM for unaccompanied housing, and increased transparency in FSRM expenditures.[[48](#fn48)] [[49]](#fn49)

# Systemic Vulnerabilities and Barrier Identification

- 1. **Pilot Production Bottlenecks**

**Primary Constraint: Legacy Trainer Fleet Obsolescence**

The T-38 Talon, first flown in 1959 and introduced in 1961, has been "life-extended multiple times," according to Brigadier General Matthew Leard, director of plans and program requirements at AETC. With 486 T-38 aircraft averaging nearly 60 years old and posting a 55% mission capable rate, the trainer fleet cannot sustain the sortie generation required to produce 1,500 pilots annually. "There's an escalating cost of retaining the airplane and keeping it flyable," Leard stated. "Additionally, it's no longer aligned with current or future aircraft." [[9](#fn9)] [[1]](#fn1)

The T-7A Red Hawk replacement program has become a case study in acquisition challenges. Despite Boeing winning the contract in 2018 with promises of digital engineering and rapid production, the program has encountered repeated design flaws, testing failures, and quality control problems. Key issues include:[[10](#fn10)] [[50](#fn50)] [[8]](#fn8)

- Ejection seat safety concerns requiring multiple redesigns
- Flight control software instabilities
- Supply chain disruptions delaying component deliveries
- Quality problems with delivered test articles forcing multi-month delays

The Air Force and Boeing restructured the acquisition strategy in January 2025, delaying the production decision (Milestone C) from FY2025 to 2026 and purchasing four additional production-representative test aircraft to increase testing capacity. Initial operational capability, originally scheduled for 2023, has slipped to November 2027—and that timeline assumes no further setbacks. Boeing aims to increase production to two aircraft per month by end of 2026, but recent history suggests skepticism is warranted.[[8](#fn8)] [[9](#fn9)] [[10]](#fn10)

**Secondary Constraint: Instructor Workforce Shortages**

Simulator instructor shortages at UPT bases force uniformed instructor pilots to backfill positions, reducing the number of experienced pilots available for operational squadrons. The FUPT program attempts to address this by outsourcing initial training to civilian contractors, but implementation challenges remain. Geographic distribution of civilian training programs—currently four locations—creates logistical complexity and requires careful timing to ensure students don't lose proficiency during the transfer from private to military training phases.[[7]](#fn7)

# Retention: The "Airline Problem"

**Structural Economic Competition**

Commercial airlines can offer compensation packages that dwarf military pay. First officers at major carriers earn $100,000-$150,000 annually in early years, with captains at legacy carriers earning $250,000-$400,000. More importantly, airline pilots enjoy dramatically superior work-life balance: predictable schedules, minimal administrative duties, union protections, and the ability to live in one location for their entire career.[[14]](#fn14)

The Air Force's enhanced retention bonuses—up to $50,000 annually or $600,000 over 12 years—attempt to bridge this compensation gap, but they address only one dimension of the

retention equation. A 2018 study by War on the Rocks found that while financial incentives matter, pilots consistently cite non-monetary factors as equally or more important: reduced additional duties, more flying opportunities, career progression transparency, and assignment stability. [25] [22]

**Operational Tempo and Burnout**

Despite AFFORGEN's promise of predictable deployment cycles, high operational tempo remains a chronic problem, particularly for high-demand, low-density specialties. Air Force Special Operations Command personnel were deploying at approximately 1:1 dwell-to-deploy ratios as recently as 2016, meaning aircrew spent as much time deployed as at home station—an unsustainable pace that "crushes" talented individuals over seven to eight years. While AFSOC negotiated with combatant commanders to reduce demand signals, relieving some pressure, contingencies in the Middle East and Indo-Pacific continue to generate high deployment requirements.[[51](#fn51)] [[52](#fn52)] [[53]](#fn53)

Combat pilots in remotely piloted aircraft face unique stressors. RPA operators conduct combat operations from stateside bases like Creech Air Force Base, Nevada, "flying" armed missions over Afghanistan or Iraq during duty hours, then returning home to families each evening—a disorienting cognitive transition. As former Air Force Chief Master Sergeant Matthew Caruso acknowledged in 2016, "We don't even have a real model to quantify" the psychological impact, noting that going home at the end of the day "doesn't mean it isn't really important that we understand the [work-life balance and psychological] impact of that kind of ops."[[51]](#fn51)

# Absorption: Squadron Capacity Constraints

The Air Force's ability to absorb new pilots into operational squadrons and develop them into experienced combat aviators is constrained by squadron size, experience ratios, and sortie availability. A squadron with insufficient experienced pilots creates a self-reinforcing problem: new pilots require more intensive mentoring and longer to reach proficiency, which increases workload on the limited cadre of experienced aviators, which accelerates burnout and attrition, which further degrades the experience ratio. [[54](#fn54)] [[11]](#fn11)

The Ready Aircrew Program mandates minimum sortie requirements to maintain combat mission ready status: inexperienced fighter pilots must fly nine sorties monthly, experienced pilots eight. However, these are minimums, and historical data shows pilots require approximately three sorties per week (12-13 monthly) to develop and maintain combat proficiency. Many squadrons struggle to provide even minimum RAP sorties due to aircraft availability constraints, simulator limitations, range access, and competing demands for maintenance and logistics resources.[[20]](#fn20)

Air Combat Command has attempted to increase fighter squadron sizes from 18 to 24 primary assigned aircraft, which would theoretically increase absorption capacity and reduce administrative overhead through consolidation. However, implementation has been hampered by lack of Base Realignment and Closure authority, which prevents the service from consolidating units onto fewer bases to achieve economies of scale.[[14]](#fn14)

# Aircraft: The Spare Parts Dilemma

Spare parts shortages stem from multiple causes:

**Supply Chain Fragmentation:** Complex, multi-tiered supply chains with single-source suppliers for critical components create vulnerabilities. When one supplier experiences quality problems or delivery delays, ripple effects cascade through the entire maintenance ecosystem. The F-35 program, with over 5,000 different part numbers managed, exemplifies this complexity.[[29](#fn29)] [[31]](#fn31)

**Insufficient Depot Capacity:** Depot-level maintenance facilities perform the most complex repairs and overhauls. The F-35 enterprise operates six U.S. military depots, but they are executing only 55% of activated repair workloads as of 2023, with full capacity not expected until 2028. This creates a backlog of components awaiting repair, which depletes the available spares pool.[[29]](#fn29)

**Inadequate Forecasting:** Predicting spare parts demand requires sophisticated modeling that accounts for operating tempo, mission profiles, environmental conditions, and component reliability trends. Historically, forecasting accuracy has been poor, resulting in either excess inventory of unneeded parts or critical shortages of high-demand items. AI-enabled systems like PANDA represent significant improvements, but implementation remains limited.[[35](#fn35)] [[36]](#fn36)

**Funding Instability:** Weapons System Sustainment accounts, which fund spare parts procurement, have been chronically underfunded. The FY2026 budget funds WSS at 85%-below the level needed to maintain current readiness, let alone improve it. This forces maintenance units to prioritize critical systems while accepting degraded availability of secondary systems, gradually eroding overall capability.[[55]](#fn55)

# Infrastructure: Decades of Underinvestment

The $49.5 billion deferred maintenance backlog represents the cumulative cost of years of prioritizing current operations and modernization over infrastructure sustainment. This creates a compound problem: deferred maintenance accelerates degradation, which increases future repair costs and eventually forces more expensive replacement rather than renovation.[[4]](#fn4)

Continuing resolutions exacerbate the challenge. CRs freeze spending at prior-year levels, preventing new construction starts, prohibiting increases to existing programs, and creating inefficiencies through short planning horizons and repeated contract restarts. A full-year CR for FY2025 would cost the Air Force between $4 billion (if anomalies are granted) and $14 billion (without anomalies and with Fiscal Responsibility Act caps). Lieutenant General Adrian Spain, deputy chief of staff for operations, testified that the service "cannot afford" this level of reduction.[[56](#fn56)] [[57]](#fn57)

The tension between current readiness and future modernization forces impossible tradeoffs. As the FY2026 Air Force posture statement acknowledges: "Investing too heavily in immediate readiness risks devastating modernization. On the other hand, investing too heavily in modernization starves foundational readiness accounts, reducing the immediate deterrent value of the DAF." Weapon System Sustainment and Flying Hour Program portfolios struggle to maintain availability rates as the fleet ages and costs rise, "compounded by our aging fleet and rising costs," according to the posture statement.[[55]](#fn55)

# Policy Recommendations: Restoring Combat Power

- 1. **Pillar 1: Pilot & Aircrew Training**

**Recommendation 1.1: Accelerate T-7A Fielding and Expand Production Capacity**

_Problem:_ T-38 obsolescence constrains pilot production; T-7A delays threaten training pipeline through 2027 and beyond.

_Policy Actions:_

- **Congressional Action:** Authorize multi-year procurement contracts for T-7A to provide Boeing with production stability and enable advance purchasing of long-lead components. Multi-year contracts typically reduce unit costs 5-10% while providing predictability that supports workforce stability and supply chain optimization.
- **SECAF/CSAF:** Direct AFLCMC to establish an integrated program office embedding Air Force personnel at Boeing's St. Louis production facility to provide real-time oversight, similar to arrangements for other major aircraft programs. Early identification and resolution of quality issues will prevent delays from cascading.
- **AETC:** Develop contingency plans to extend T-38 service life through selective component upgrades focused on flight-critical systems, accepting that the aircraft will never match T-7 capability but ensuring training can continue if further T-7 delays occur.

_Expected Outcomes:_ IOC by November 2027 becomes achievable rather than aspirational; risk of training pipeline collapse reduced; production rate reaches 24 aircraft annually by 2028, enabling retirement of the T-38 fleet by early 2030s.

**Recommendation 1.2: Expand and Refine the FUPT Model**

_Problem:_ Government-owned training infrastructure and uniformed instructor availability constrain throughput.

_Policy Actions:_

- **AETC:** Award contracts to at least two additional civilian flying training providers (total of six) to increase geographic distribution, reduce student wait times, and create competitive pressure for quality and throughput. Geographic diversity also provides resilience against weather disruptions or localized facility problems.
- **HAF A3:** Establish performance-based contracts with civilian training providers that incentivize on-time graduation rates, first-time checkride pass rates, and student safety records. Structure payments to reward excellence rather than simply seat-time.
- **AETC:** Reduce transfer time between civilian initial pilot training and military UPT phases from the current three weeks to no more than 10 days to minimize skill degradation. Position Government-Furnished Equipment at civilian training sites to enable students to begin military administrative processing while completing civilian training.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Pilot production reaches sustained 1,500 annually by FY2027; waiting time between commissioning and training start reduced by 60-90 days; cost per pilot declines as competitive market develops.

**Recommendation 1.3: Increase Absorption Capacity Through Squadron Modernization**

_Problem:_ Squadrons lack capacity to absorb new pilots while maintaining healthy experience ratios and operational commitments.

_Policy Actions:_

- **Congress:** Authorize and fund Air Combat Command's initiative to increase fighter squadron sizes from 18 to 24 primary assigned aircraft across all active duty fighter squadrons over a five-year period. Budget approximately $6 billion annually for incremental aircraft procurement (combination of F-15EX, F-35A, and advanced F-16 variants).
- **ACC:** Restructure fighter squadron manning to 1.50 pilots per aircraft (increased from 1.25) to better align with combat requirements and provide margin for training and administrative duties. This requires growing the pilot corps by approximately 300 pilots above current targets, but provides the depth needed to sustain operations.
- **AFRC/ANG:** Expand the 495th Fighter Group Total Force Integration model to at least three additional locations, leveraging Reserve Component instructor pilot experience to increase active duty pilot absorption capacity. Budget for approximately 200 additional ARC instructor pilot positions.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Squadron experience ratios return to optimal 60% experienced within four years; monthly sortie rates increase by 30-40%; deployment frequency decreases as larger squadrons provide more personnel to distribute workload.

**Recommendation 1.4: Implement Comprehensive Retention Strategy**

_Problem:_ Financial incentives alone cannot overcome quality-of-life and quality-of-service concerns driving pilot departures.

_Policy Actions:_

- **SECAF:** Establish a "fly-only" career track for pilots who wish to remain in operational cockpits without competing for command or staff positions. Structure this track with clear promotion pathways to O-5 and selective O-6, with compensation tied to continued flying proficiency rather than traditional staff metrics. Pilot this concept with fighter pilots first, then expand to other communities.
- **HAF A1:** Increase contractor and Government Service civilian positions in fighter squadrons by 50% over three years to absorb additional duties currently performed by pilots. Specifically target scheduling, training records management, and operational planning support functions. Budget approximately $250 million annually for 1,500 additional support positions.
- **ACC:** Mandate that no pilot in a line flying position may hold more than two primary additional duties, with those duties specifically limited to mission-essential functions (e.g.,

weapons officer, flight safety officer). Transfer all other duties to commander support staff or mission support group personnel.

- **HAF A1/A3:** Guarantee assignment location preference for pilots who extend beyond their initial commitment by at least five years and maintain instructor or evaluator qualifications. Create assignment preference tiers: first-look goes to pilots with 15+ years of service, second-look to those with 10-15 years, ensuring senior aviators can provide stability for their families while remaining in flying positions.
- **SECAF/CSAF:** Request legislative authority for targeted mid-career sabbaticals (6-12 months) for pilots with 8-12 years of service, allowing temporary separation for family needs or personal development with guaranteed return to flying positions. This provides a retention mechanism for pilots facing family pressures who might otherwise separate permanently.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Pilot retention rates for pilots with 10-13 years of service increase from current ~35% to ~50% within three years; experienced pilot inventory stabilizes; airline competition remains but is partially offset by improved QOL/QOS.

**Recommendation 1.5: Optimize Training Efficiency Through Technology**

_Problem:_ Limited aircraft availability and instructor capacity create training bottlenecks. _Policy Actions:_

- **ACC/AETC:** Accelerate implementation of high-fidelity distributed mission training simulators that enable squadron-level training from home station, reducing deployment to ranges for basic tactical scenarios. Invest $500 million over three years to upgrade simulators across all fighter bases.
- **ACC:** Revise Ready Aircrew Program requirements to credit high-fidelity simulator events at higher rates for fundamental tasks (instrument approaches, emergency procedures, basic tactical maneuvers), reserving live flight hours for scenarios requiring actual flight dynamics or live weapons employment.
- **AFMC:** Complete SCARS implementation across all 2,400 simulators by FY2028 (accelerated from current FY2030 timeline), enabling networked training where multiple squadrons train together in synthetic environment regardless of physical location.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Effective monthly sortie capacity increases by 20% through optimal blending of live and simulated training; reduced wear on aircraft extends service life; training quality improves through more frequent mission rehearsal.

# Pillar 2: Aircraft Readiness

**Recommendation 2.1: Fully Fund Weapons System Sustainment**

_Problem:_ Chronic WSS underfunding drives spare parts shortages and cascading readiness failures.

_Policy Actions:_

- **Congress:** Increase WSS appropriation from current 85% to 100% of validated requirements beginning in FY2027, representing an additional $3-4 billion annually.

Structure funding as a separate title within the appropriations bill to prevent internal reprogramming.

- **SAF/FM:** Implement zero-based budgeting review of WSS requirements for top 20 aircraft platforms (by total inventory) to ensure requirement calculations reflect actual operational tempo, mission profiles, and age-related degradation. Update sustainment models annually rather than every 2-3 years to account for changing conditions.
- **AFMC:** Establish WSS as a protected account that cannot be taxed for "end-of-year funding emergencies" or reprogrammed without SECAF personal approval, preventing the erosion that historically occurs when other programs encounter shortfalls.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Mission capable rates increase 5-8 percentage points within 18 months as spare parts inventories rebuild; cannibalization rates decrease by 40%; maintenance man-hours per flight hour decline by 10-15% as technicians have parts available rather than waiting.

**Recommendation 2.2: Accelerate F-35 Supply Chain Improvements**

_Problem:_ F-35 readiness lags all other fighters despite being newest aircraft; depot capacity and spare parts availability insufficient.

_Policy Actions:_

- **F-35 JPO:** Accelerate depot activation schedule to achieve full operational capability by FY2026 (two years earlier than current FY2028 target). Budget additional $800 million over two years for facility upgrades, tooling, training, and contractor support to compress timeline.
- **SAF/AQ:** Renegotiate F-35 sustainment contracts to implement true performance-based logistics, where Lockheed Martin and Pratt & Whitney receive bonus payments for exceeding mission capable rate targets and incur penalties for failing to meet them. Structure with quarterly assessment cycles to drive rapid improvement.
- **DLA:** Complete migration of all F-35 spare parts from commercial warehouses to DLA facilities positioned at or near F-35 bases by end of FY2026. Co-locate DLA personnel with squadron maintenance supervision to enable real-time response to emerging part requirements.
- **AFMC:** Establish Government-owned depot repair capability for F-35 propulsion systems and avionics at Ogden Air Logistics Complex and Warner Robins Air Logistics Complex to reduce dependence on contractor depots and improve responsiveness. Budget $1.2 billion for facility modifications and equipment.

_Expected Outcomes:_ F-35A mission capable rate increases from 51.5% to 70% by FY2028; awaiting parts time decreases from days to hours for critical components; lifecycle costs decline as organic depot capability provides competitive pressure on contractor pricing.

**Recommendation 2.3: Expand Predictive Maintenance Across the Fleet**

_Problem:_ Current maintenance practices are reactive rather than predictive, resulting in excess unscheduled maintenance and inefficient parts utilization.

_Policy Actions:_

- **AFMC Rapid Sustainment Office:** Scale PANDA deployment from current 16 platforms to all combat-coded aircraft platforms by FY2027. Prioritize based on: (1) platforms with lowest mission capable rates, (2) platforms with highest operational demand, (3) platforms with most complex maintenance requirements. Budget $150 million for expansion.
- **HAF A4:** Mandate that all new aircraft acquisitions include AI-ready data architecture and sensor suites specifically designed to support predictive maintenance algorithms from initial operational capability. Update aircraft specifications to require contractor-provided predictive maintenance models as part of initial delivery.
- **AFMC:** Establish Machine Learning Operations (MLOps) capability within Air Force Sustainment Center to continuously improve predictive maintenance algorithms based on operational data, enabling faster refinement than current contractor-dependent model.
- **SAF/AQ:** Negotiate data rights agreements with all aircraft original equipment manufacturers to provide the Air Force with full access to engineering drawings, failure mode analysis, and component reliability data required to develop government-owned predictive models, reducing dependence on proprietary contractor systems.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Unscheduled maintenance decreases 30-40% for aircraft covered by mature predictive models; spare parts inventory optimized, reducing excess inventory 15-20% while improving availability of critical items; maintenance workforce utilization improves as maintenance actions concentrate on highest-priority items.

**Recommendation 2.4: Bridge Fourth and Fifth Generation Readiness Through F-15EX**

_Problem:_ F-35 production insufficient to replace retiring legacy fighters; F-22 fleet limited to 180 aircraft; capability gap emerging.

_Policy Actions:_

- **Congress:** Support increased F-15EX procurement from current 129 program of record to at least 180 aircraft, providing inventory depth and operational flexibility. The FY2026 proposal to increase from 98 to 129 represents progress, but additional growth is warranted given capability gaps.
- **SAF/AQ:** Accelerate F-15EX delivery rate from current ~14 per year to Boeing's target of 24 per year by 2027 through advance procurement funding for long-lead items. Structure contract to incentivize early delivery.
- **ACC:** Employ F-15EX primarily for homeland defense, cruise missile interdiction, and permissive environment strike missions, reserving F-35 for operations in contested environments against integrated air defense systems. This task-based allocation optimizes the use of higher-cost, lower-inventory 5th generation assets.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Combat-coded fighter inventory stabilizes rather than continuing to decline; experienced F-15C/D pilots transition to F-15EX with minimal retraining time (approximately 6 weeks vs. 9 months for new type); operational readiness maintained during transition without capability gap.

**Recommendation 2.5: Manage Bomber Transition to Prevent Capability Gaps**

_Problem:_ B-1 retirement before B-21 IOC creates dangerous "bathtub effect" reducing bomber capacity up to 30%.

_Policy Actions:_

- **ACC:** Delay B-1B retirement until at least 12 months after first B-21 squadron reaches Initial Operational Capability. Reprogram $400 million for B-1 sustainment through FY2028 to enable this extension.
- **AFGSC:** Co-locate B-1B and B-21 at Ellsworth Air Force Base during transition period, utilizing space created by previous divestiture of 17 B-1s. This enables experienced B-1 crews to transition gradually to B-21 while maintaining bomber capacity.
- **SAF/AQ:** Accelerate B-21 low-rate initial production to deliver 6 aircraft annually (up from current 4) by FY2028, compressing IOC timeline and reducing overlap period where both platforms require sustainment.
- **HAF A3:** Conduct comprehensive mission analysis to identify B-1 capabilities that B-21 and B-52 cannot replicate (specifically high-speed, low-altitude penetration and large

conventional payload), then develop operational concepts that either transfer these missions to other platforms or accept managed risk during transition.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Bomber-available aircraft to combatant commands remains stable throughout transition period; aircrew training continuity maintained; risk of capability gap during high-tension period with China mitigated.

**Recommendation 2.6: Strengthen Maintenance Workforce Pipeline**

_Problem:_ Maintenance workforce experience gap constrains aircraft availability; civilian sector competition threatens retention.

_Policy Actions:_

- **HAF A1:** Increase Selective Retention Bonus allocations for undermanned maintenance specialties by $500 million annually to match civilian sector compensation more closely. Target bonuses specifically to 5-level and 7-level maintainers with 6-12 years of service.
- **AETC:** Expand maintenance technical training capacity at Sheppard Air Force Base and other technical training centers by 20%, enabling throughput increase of 2,000-3,000 additional maintainers annually. Budget $250 million for additional instructors, simulators, and training equipment.
- **HAF A1:** Establish "emeritus maintainer" program enabling recently retired maintainers to return to government service as civilian instructors at technical training centers or as augmentees to undermanned maintenance squadrons, leveraging their experience while providing post-military employment. Target 500 positions within two years.
- **AFMC:** Expand virtual reality maintenance training systems from current Air National Guard implementation to all active duty maintenance training squadrons. Budget $100 million to procure 500 additional VR systems covering top 15 aircraft platforms. VR training enables continuation during weather delays and aircraft unavailability while freeing actual aircraft for operational missions.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Maintenance manning reaches 100% of requirements within four years; experience ratios normalize as retention improves; training capacity increases enable absorption of larger recruiting classes; maintenance-related mission aborts decrease 25%.

# Pillar 3: Infrastructure

**Recommendation 3.1: Establish Dedicated Deferred Maintenance Reduction Fund**

_Problem:_ $49.5B backlog accumulates faster than current FSRM funding can address; facilities continue to deteriorate.

_Policy Actions:_

- **Congress:** Authorize and appropriate a dedicated Deferred Maintenance Reduction Fund of $5 billion annually for five years (total $25 billion), separate from base FSRM accounts and protected from reprogramming. Structure fund with specific metrics: 50% must be spent on mission-critical facilities (hangars, maintenance facilities, operational buildings), 30% on quality-of-life facilities (dormitories, fitness centers, dining facilities), and 20% on supporting infrastructure (utilities, roads, parking).
- **SAF/IE:** Conduct comprehensive facilities assessment across all Air Force installations to prioritize backlog projects based on: (1) mission impact, (2) safety risk, (3) cost-to-delay ratio (projects where deferral increases future cost), (4) readiness impact. Publish prioritized project list with transparent methodology.
- **SAF/FM:** Implement "fix it right" policy mandating that when deferred maintenance projects are finally executed, work must bring facilities to modern standards rather than simply restoring to original condition. While more expensive initially, this prevents rapid re-accumulation of backlog and improves energy efficiency and resilience.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Deferred maintenance backlog reduced by 50% over five years; facility condition index improves from current degraded state to acceptable standards; airmen quality of life improves measurably; energy costs decline through modernization.

**Recommendation 3.2: Modernize Training Ranges for High-End Fight**

_Problem:_ Training ranges insufficient to support realistic contested environment training against peer adversary tactics.

_Policy Actions:_

- **ACC:** Invest $2 billion over five years to upgrade threat simulation capabilities at Nellis, Eglin, Mountain Home, and Eielson ranges with systems that accurately replicate Chinese and Russian integrated air defense systems, including S-400, S-500, and HQ-9 radar and missile characteristics.
- **AFMC:** Develop transportable threat emitter systems that can deploy to exercises at any location, providing realistic training without requiring permanent infrastructure investment at every base. Budget $300 million for initial procurement of 20 systems.
- **ACC:** Expand low-altitude training airspace capacity near fighter bases by 40% through aggressive engagement with FAA and local communities to adjust airspace boundaries.

Low-altitude training is essential for penetrating strike missions but faces community resistance; employ professional outreach and economic impact studies to make the case.

- **SAF/AQ:** Accelerate SCARS implementation (as noted in 1.5) while simultaneously resolving cybersecurity risk management framework obstacles that currently halt funding. Direct SAF/CN (Chief Information Officer) to establish streamlined cybersecurity certification process for training systems that accepts higher risk than operational systems warrant.

_Expected Outcomes:_ Fighter squadrons achieve 90% of required training without deploying away from home station, reducing TDY burden; training more accurately reflects combat environment, improving survivability rates; adversary air capabilities expand, providing more realistic opposition force training.

**Recommendation 3.3: Address Quality-of-Life Infrastructure Deficiencies**

_Problem:_ Inadequate housing, child care, and family support facilities undermine retention and readiness.

_Policy Actions:_

- **Congress:** Fully fund unaccompanied housing FSRM at 100% of requirements ($1.2 billion annually) and increase BAH to 100% of actual housing costs in all markets (eliminating current underfunding in high-cost areas). BAH underfunding forces service members to supplement housing costs out of pocket, creating financial hardship.
- **SAF/IE:** Accelerate Child Development Center construction and renovation to eliminate waitlists at all installations within three years. Budget $800 million for facility construction and $400 million annually for additional operating costs. Insufficient child care forces families to choose between career and family, with retention consequences.
- **HAF A1:** Expand dependent care flexible spending account eligibility and increase maximum contribution from $5,000 to $10,000 annually, recognizing that child care costs in many markets exceed current limits. Work with Congress to authorize the increase.
- **SAF/IE:** Implement remote installation assessment program (as announced in 2024) to evaluate conditions at isolated bases and develop targeted improvement plans. Prioritize installations with highest adverse quality-of-life indicators (climate extremes, geographic isolation, limited medical/educational/recreational facilities).

_Expected Outcomes:_ Unaccompanied housing occupant satisfaction increases from current

~65% to >85%; child care waitlists eliminated, removing barrier to dual-military and single-parent retention; housing cost burden decreases measurably.

**Recommendation 3.4: Optimize Capital Investment Through MILCON Prioritization**

_Problem:_ Limited MILCON budget must balance beddown of new weapon systems, infrastructure modernization, and quality-of-life improvements.

_Policy Actions:_

- **SAF/IE:** Establish three-tier MILCON priority system: Tier 1 (50% of budget) - mission-critical facilities for new weapon systems and nuclear modernization (F-47, Sentinel, B-21, SAOC); Tier 2 (30% of budget) - readiness-critical maintenance and operational facilities;

Tier 3 (20% of budget) - quality-of-life facilities. Enforce discipline in priority execution, preventing Tier 3 projects from consuming Tier 1/2 resources.

- **Congress:** Appropriate FY2026 MILCON request of $3.8 billion in full, without reducing below requested level. MILCON is one account where underfunding causes program delays that increase costs and defer critical capabilities.
- **SAF/IE:** Implement modular construction techniques for facilities where appropriate (dormitories, administrative buildings, some maintenance facilities), reducing construction time 30-40% and cost 15-20% while maintaining quality. Establish partnerships with leading modular construction firms to develop Air Force-specific building systems.

_Expected Outcomes:_ MILCON project delivery time reduced from current average 48 months to 36 months; cost growth contained to <5% rather than historical 15-20%; weapon system beddown delays minimized.

# Funding Strategy

Three primary funding mechanisms can support these investments:

- - 1. **Budget Baseline Growth:** Request sustained 3-4% real growth in Air Force topline for FY2027-FY2031, above inflation. This represents largest share of required funding and aligns with bipartisan recognition that defense budget must grow to address pacing threat.
    2. **Modernization Reallocation:** Carefully selected program terminations or reductions can free resources for readiness investments. Candidates include:
       - Reduce planned F-35A procurement from historical levels to 42 annually (as in FY2026 proposal), with savings redirected to F-15EX and sustainment
       - Delay lower-priority modernization programs 2-3 years to concentrate resources on readiness foundation
    3. **Operational Efficiencies:** Predictive maintenance, reduced TDY through distributed training, and improved supply chain management will generate savings that partially offset investment requirements. Conservative estimate: $500M-$1B annually by FY2029.

# Risk Analysis and Mitigating Factors

- 1. **Primary Risks**

**Budget Constraints**

_Risk:_ Congress fails to appropriate requested funding levels; continuing resolutions limit flexibility.

_Mitigation:_ Structure recommendations with tiered priority so that partial funding still delivers meaningful improvement. Establish protected accounts for highest-priority items (WSS, T-7A, pilot retention) that cannot be reprogrammed. Demonstrate fiscal responsibility through efficiency initiatives that show the Air Force is managing current resources effectively.

**Program Execution Challenges**

_Risk:_ T-7A continues to experience delays; F-35 supply chain improvements fail to materialize; contractor performance lags expectations.

_Mitigation:_ Build schedule margin into all timelines; establish government program offices with direct oversight authority; include contractual penalties for non-performance with sufficient magnitude to drive contractor behavior; develop alternative paths (e.g., extended T-38 service) for critical programs.

**Workforce Availability**

_Risk:_ Commercial aviation and civilian maintenance sector competition intensifies, overwhelming financial incentives.

_Mitigation:_ Focus on non-monetary quality-of-life improvements that civilian sector cannot easily replicate (predictable assignments, meaningful mission, advanced technology exposure).

Recognize that some attrition is inevitable and ensure pipeline capacity exceeds steady-state requirements by 10-15% to account for expected losses.

**Geopolitical Contingencies**

_Risk:_ Major conflict in Indo-Pacific or Middle East forces immediate surge deployment, disrupting training pipeline and accelerating equipment wear.

_Mitigation:_ Structure force generation model (AFFORGEN) to maintain strategic reserve capacity; preposition equipment to reduce deployment requirements; accelerate Collaborative Combat Aircraft development to provide surge capacity without requiring additional pilots.

# Strategic Opportunities

**Technological Leverage**

Artificial intelligence, advanced manufacturing (3D printing for parts), and virtual training environments offer potential to deliver more capability at lower cost than traditional approaches. The Air Force should aggressively pursue these opportunities while maintaining realistic expectations about implementation timelines.

**Allied Partnerships**

NATO allies and Indo-Pacific partners face similar pilot training and aircraft maintenance challenges. Collaborative initiatives in training, supply chain management, and maintenance could reduce per-unit costs while strengthening interoperability. The F-35 global enterprise demonstrates this model, though implementation has been imperfect.

**Industrial Base Expansion**

Current concentration of critical suppliers creates vulnerability. Deliberately cultivating additional suppliers for key components—accepting near-term costs to develop competition—improves long-term resilience and reduces pricing power of incumbent contractors.

# Conclusion

The United States Air Force faces a readiness crisis characterized by historically low aircraft availability, chronic pilot shortages, and crumbling infrastructure. With mission capable rates at 62%—the lowest in recent memory—and nearly 1,900 aircraft unavailable on any given day, the service's ability to execute its primary mission of global power projection is in jeopardy. The pilot shortage of 1,850 airmen, persistent for over a decade, shows no signs of resolution under current trajectories. A $49.5 billion deferred maintenance backlog threatens both current operations and future capability.[[2](#fn2)] [[4](#fn4)] [[1]](#fn1)

These challenges stem from decades of deferred investment, compounded by an aging fleet averaging 32 years old, acquisition program delays, and structural factors beyond Air Force

control—particularly commercial airline demand for pilots and private sector competition for maintenance technicians. Left unaddressed, these trends will accelerate. Aircraft will become progressively harder to maintain as they age. The pilot crisis will worsen as production lags attrition and commercial airlines continue aggressive hiring. Infrastructure will deteriorate beyond

economical repair, forcing eventual replacement at multiples of current maintenance costs.[16]
